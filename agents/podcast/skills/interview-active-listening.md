# Interview Active Listening

**Tags:** podcast, interview, listening, conversation, technique

## Core Principle
The best podcast interviews happen when the host disappears into genuine curiosity — following what the guest actually says rather than what the next question card says.

## The Fundamental Problem
Most interview hosts listen for their turn to speak. Active listening means listening for what is *surprising*, *unresolved*, or *contradictory* in what the guest just said — and following that thread before moving on.

## Techniques

**The Pregnant Pause**
After a guest finishes a thought, wait 2–3 beats before responding. Silence feels longer than it is, and guests almost always fill it with the most honest, unguarded thing they were holding back.

**The "Tell Me More" Reflex**
When a guest says something that surprises you, your first instinct is to move to the next question. Override it. "You just said X — I didn't expect that. Say more." This is where the best material lives.

**Signaling vs. Leading**
- Signal understanding: "So what you're saying is..." (reflect back to confirm, not to steer)
- Don't lead: "So what you're saying is [your interpretation that subtly changes their meaning]..."

**Question Discipline**
- One question at a time. Multi-part questions let guests answer the easiest part and skip the hard one.
- Short questions get longer answers. Long questions signal that you want to hear yourself talk.
- Best follow-up: "Why?" — deceptively simple, generates depth.

**Preparation Paradox**
Prepare thoroughly so you can ignore your notes. Deep preparation means you can deviate from the script without losing the thread — you know where the conversation needs to end up and can find multiple paths there.

## What to Listen For
- **Contradictions**: between what they're saying now and what they said 10 minutes ago
- **Emotion**: a change in tone or pace signals something matters to them
- **The unsaid**: what question did they answer but avoid the implications of?
