# Episode Arc Structure

**Tags:** podcast, structure, storytelling, narrative, scripting, editing

## Core Principle
Structure is the sequence of cognitive mode changes that makes the listener's attention self-sustaining. If you remove the structure, the content might survive as a blog post but dies as audio.

## Act 1 — Hook and Frame (0–10% of runtime)
- Find the most *unresolved* 20–60 seconds from deep in the conversation. Cut it to the front. This is your cold open.
- State the episode's central question within 90 seconds of the cold open ending. **Delete test**: remove everything before minute 2. If the listener loses nothing, you're throat-clearing.
- If your opening contains the words "today we're going to" or any guest biography, cut it. The frame IS the content.

## Act 2 — Development (10–85% of runtime)
Structure around 3–5 *territory shifts*: not topic changes, but cognitive mode changes.

**Plant-and-payoff method**: During your first listen-through, timecode-stamp any specific detail (a name, a number, an anecdote) introduced in the first third. Before final mix, check: does at least one of those details recur in the final third with new significance? If nothing from minute 10 matters at minute 40, the episode is a list, not an arc.

**Plateau test**: Play any 5-minute segment. Two diagnostics:
1. **Swap test**: Can you move this segment before or after its neighbor without the listener noticing? If yes, it has no structural function.
2. **Energy band test**: In the waveform, does the segment's average amplitude stay within 2dB of the surrounding segments? Flat energy for 5+ minutes signals a plateau even when the content feels substantive.

Fix with one of:
1. **Cut it.** Most plateaus are the guest restating what they already said in different words. Cut to the single best articulation.
2. **Reorder it.** Move the segment to where it creates maximum contrast with adjacent material — pair a quiet analytical section against a high-energy anecdote.
3. **Interrupt it.** Insert a counter-example, a question pivot, or a 1.5-second silence. Silence after sustained talking is the cheapest energy reset in audio.

## Act 3 — Resolution and Resonance (85–100%)
- Return to the Act 1 question explicitly. The listener must feel the circle close.
- No new information after the 85% mark. New facts here belong earlier or in another episode.
- **Script your last 60 seconds.** Write it out word-for-word. Improvised endings almost always underperform written ones. **Last-line test**: read your final 15 seconds aloud. An ending contains a shift in register — a generalization, a return to a specific image, or a deliberate silence. If it sounds like you ran out of things to say, rewrite it.

## Edit Moves (named techniques)
- **The Cold Open Splice**: *Trigger: your episode starts with context-setting, introductions, or any sentence a listener would skip.* Find the most charged 20–60 seconds from deep in the conversation. Cut it to the front. Add 1.5 seconds of silence or a brief musical sting as transition, then start the episode properly.
- **The Contrast Cut**: *Trigger: two adjacent segments have similar energy levels in the waveform — both loud, both quiet, or both mid-range for 3+ minutes each.* Insert a 2-second silence or a tonal shift (music, ambient sound, a different voice) between them. Alternatively, reorder so a high-energy segment follows a low-energy one.
- **The Callback**: *Trigger: a guest says something offhand in the first 15 minutes that connects to something said 15+ minutes later.* Note both timecodes during your first pass. In the edit, add a brief re-reference ("remember when you said X — is this what you meant?") or, if the guest doesn't make the connection, add it as a scripted host line in post.
- **The Mercy Kill**: *Trigger: a segment runs longer than 7 minutes without a territory shift, OR the waveform shows flat amplitude for 5+ minutes.* Cut it to its best 90 seconds. The information can survive compression; the energy cannot survive length.
- **The Energy Reset**: *Trigger: three consecutive segments trend in the same energy direction (all escalating or all de-escalating).* Insert a segment that breaks the trend: a quiet moment after three intense ones, or a punchy anecdote after three reflective ones. If you don't have the material, a 2-second silence with a music bed change accomplishes the same reset.
- **The Off-Script Interruption**: *Trigger: guest's vocal pitch drops into a narrow, even band for 3+ minutes, signaling they've shifted from reliving to reciting.* Interrupt with a specific follow-up that forces them off-script: "What were you feeling when that happened?" or "Who disagreed with you?" Edit the question out, leaving only the fresh response.
- **The Counterargument Injection**: *Trigger: affirmation words ("exactly," "right," "totally") appear 4+ times in a 10-minute block.* The host must voice the strongest available disagreement, even if they ultimately agree. Edit to present it as a natural challenge, not an argument.
- **The Transcript Test**: *Trigger: host speaks for less than 5% of a 10-minute stretch.* Mute the audio and read the transcript of that block. If the text alone doesn't compel you, the episode is riding vocal performance. Add at least 2 editorial interruptions (questions, clarifications) in that block during the edit.
- **The Pause Cut**: *Trigger: a long pause (3+ seconds) followed by the guest restarting with higher energy and different cadence.* The guest just said the thing they actually wanted to say. In the edit, cut everything before the pause. The restart is the real take.
- **The Dependency Link**: *Trigger: you can swap two segments without the listener noticing.* Make each unit depend on the previous one. Unit 2 should only make sense because Unit 1 established something. If you can't create dependency, cut to the single strongest unit.
- **The Ending Script**: *Trigger: average speaking rate increases 20%+ in the final 10% of runtime.* The ending is under-allocated. Stop editing. Script the last 60 seconds word-for-word, then re-edit the final segment to match that script, extending runtime if necessary.
- **The Pre-Call Reset**: *Trigger: your best material (for cold opens, key insights) consistently comes from the back third of recordings.* The guest is warming up on tape. For the next episode, start recording 15 minutes before you "start recording," or do a pre-call and throw away the first conversation. Record the second.
- **The Interview Spine**: *Trigger: you're editing an interview and the guest's vocal pitch stays in a conversational range with no emotional spikes.* They're reciting, not reliving. Apply the spine: Who they are → what they built → what broke → what they learned. Fix: by minute 15, ask (or edit in) the question "what almost didn't work?" This forces re-entry into the memory rather than the rehearsed version.
- **The Narrative Spine**: *Trigger: you're editing a narrative piece and every sentence starts with "and then."* Chronological order is the enemy of narrative. Apply the spine: Scene → complication → rising stakes → climax → denouement. Fix: start in medias res. Earn the backstory by making the listener need it. Cut any exposition that isn't demanded by the current moment's tension.
- **The Solo Spine**: *Trigger: you're editing a monologue and the speaker never steelmans an opponent.* Monologue becomes lecture without a second voice. Apply the spine: Claim → strongest objection → evidence → revised claim. Fix: voice the objection as if a skeptic is in the room — change your vocal register to mark it. Edit in a pause before the rebuttal to signal the shift.
- **The Panel Spine**: *Trigger: speakers respond only to the moderator's question, not to each other.* Listen for whether speaker 2 references speaker 1's specific words. Apply the spine: Central question → position A vs B → synthesis. Fix: after each response, edit the next speaker's intro to respond to what was just said, not the original question. If needed, script a host line to bridge: "[Speaker 2], how do you respond to [Speaker 1's specific point]?"

## The 1-Sentence Test
Summarize the episode as: "[Subject] believed/did/wanted X, until Y forced Z."
The sentence must contain a transformation or tension. "We talked to [guest] about [topic]" is a description, not an arc — it means the episode doesn't have one yet. If you can't write the sentence before you start editing, you don't yet know what the episode is about.
