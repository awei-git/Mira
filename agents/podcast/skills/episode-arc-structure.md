# Episode Arc Structure

**Tags:** podcast, structure, storytelling, narrative, scripting

## Core Principle
Every episode needs a beginning that hooks, a middle that builds, and an end that pays off — even a casual conversation episode has a structural arc that can be shaped in the edit.

## The Three-Act Episode
**Act 1 — Hook and Frame (0–10% of runtime)**
- Open with the most compelling moment, question, or claim — not with introductions.
- Frame the episode's central question: what will the listener know/feel/understand by the end that they don't now?
- Don't explain what you're about to do — do it.

**Act 2 — Development (10–85% of runtime)**
- For interview episodes: move through 3–5 distinct territory shifts (not just topics but modes: background → tension → turning point → consequence → reflection)
- For narrative episodes: build through complication and rising stakes toward a climax
- Plant and pay off: introduce a detail early that becomes significant later
- Avoid the "plateau": if energy and tension are flat for more than 5 minutes, the episode needs a cut or a structural reorder

**Act 3 — Resolution and Resonance (85–100%)**
- Answer the question framed in Act 1 — or deliberately subvert it with something better
- The ending must feel earned: the last thing the listener hears should be the most resonant, not the most convenient
- No new information in the last 5% — only synthesis

## Types of Episode Structures
- **Interview**: guest-arc structure (who they are → what they do → why it matters → what's next)
- **Narrative/documentary**: scene-based, chronological or in medias res
- **Solo/monologue**: argument-based (claim → evidence → objection → resolution)
- **Panel/roundtable**: thesis-driven (anchor discussion to a central question, not to a round-robin of opinions)

## The 1-Sentence Test
Can you summarize the episode in one sentence that contains a tension or transformation? "X believed Y until Z changed everything." If not, the episode may not have a clear arc.
