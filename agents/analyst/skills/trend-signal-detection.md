# Trend Signal Detection

**Tags:** market-analysis, trends, forecasting, signal-detection

## Core Principle
Separate weak signals of genuine emerging trends from noise by evaluating convergence across independent sources, distinguishing leading from lagging indicators, and assessing structural drivers over anecdotal evidence.

## Technique

### 1. Signal vs. Noise Framework
Not every data point is a signal. Apply these filters:

- **Frequency** -- is the pattern appearing once or repeatedly? A single article is noise. The same theme surfacing in earnings calls, patent filings, and hiring patterns is a signal.
- **Independence** -- are the sources independent? Five articles citing the same original report is one signal, not five. Look for convergence across unrelated sources (e.g., a regulatory filing + a startup funding round + a consumer behavior shift all pointing the same direction).
- **Structural basis** -- can you identify a causal mechanism? A trend needs a "why" -- technology cost curves, demographic shifts, regulatory changes, behavioral changes. If you can't identify the structural driver, treat it as unconfirmed.

### 2. Leading vs. Lagging Indicators
Sequence matters enormously. Arrange indicators from earliest to latest:

**Leading (early signals):**
- Patent filings and R&D investment shifts
- Venture capital deal flow into a category
- Talent migration patterns (where are top engineers/execs moving?)
- Regulatory proposals and government R&D priorities
- Academic publication clusters
- Early-adopter community activity (forums, GitHub stars, niche subreddits)

**Coincident (confirming signals):**
- Startup revenue growth in the category
- Enterprise pilot programs
- Industry conference keynote themes
- Analyst report coverage initiation

**Lagging (the trend is already established):**
- Mass media coverage
- Large incumbent product launches
- Market size reports from major research firms
- MBA case studies

When you see mostly lagging indicators, the trend is mature. When you see leading indicators converging without lagging confirmation, you may have found an early signal worth investigating.

### 3. The Convergence Test
A genuine emerging trend typically shows convergence across at least three of these domains:
- **Technology** -- a capability is becoming feasible or dramatically cheaper.
- **Demand** -- consumer or enterprise behavior is shifting.
- **Regulation** -- policy is enabling or forcing change.
- **Economics** -- unit economics or cost structures are crossing thresholds.
- **Culture** -- social norms or expectations are shifting.

One-domain signals are speculative. Two-domain signals are worth monitoring. Three-domain convergence is a strong trend candidate.

### 4. Tempo and Trajectory
For each identified signal, assess:
- **Speed** -- how fast is this developing? (Months, years, decades?)
- **Acceleration** -- is the rate of change increasing or plateauing?
- **Adoption curve position** -- innovators, early adopters, early majority?
- **Reversibility** -- could this trend reverse? What would cause that?

### 5. Counter-Signal Discipline
Actively seek disconfirming evidence. For every trend hypothesis, ask:
- What would make this trend fail or reverse?
- Who has an incentive to hype this trend beyond its substance?
- What are the structural barriers to adoption?
- Has a similar trend been predicted before and failed? Why?

## Application

When analyzing trends in a market:
1. Gather data points from diverse, independent sources. Prioritize primary sources (filings, data, patents) over secondary (articles, reports).
2. Classify each data point as leading, coincident, or lagging.
3. Run the convergence test -- how many independent domains show movement?
4. Assess tempo and trajectory to estimate timing.
5. Apply counter-signal discipline to stress-test the trend.
6. Present findings with explicit confidence levels: "strong signal" (3+ domain convergence, leading indicators present), "emerging signal" (2-domain, mostly leading), or "weak/speculative" (single-domain, could be noise).

Never present a trend without stating what would disprove it. Unfalsifiable trend claims are not analysis.
