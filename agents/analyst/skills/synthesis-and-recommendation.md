# Synthesis and Recommendation

**Tags:** market-analysis, strategy, decision-making, communication, insights

## Core Principle
Transform raw analysis into a decision-ready structure: state what you found, what it means, what to do about it, and what could make you wrong -- in that order.

## Technique

### 1. The Synthesis Pyramid

Structure findings from conclusion to evidence, not the reverse. Decision-makers need the answer first, then the supporting logic for those who want to verify.

**Layer 1 -- Headline finding** (one sentence)
What is the single most important takeaway? Force yourself to commit to a position. "The market is growing" is not a finding. "Company X has 18 months before margin compression forces a strategic pivot" is.

**Layer 2 -- Supporting arguments** (3-5 points)
The independent lines of reasoning that support the headline. Each should stand on its own. If removing one argument collapses the entire thesis, the finding is under-supported.

**Layer 3 -- Evidence** (data, sources, analysis)
The specific data points, calculations, and sources behind each argument. This layer exists for verification, not persuasion.

### 2. The So-What Chain

Every piece of analysis must pass the "so what" test, applied repeatedly:

- "Market X is growing at 15% CAGR." -- So what?
- "This means the addressable opportunity doubles in 5 years." -- So what?
- "Current capacity investment is tracking below what's needed to serve that demand." -- So what?
- "There is a supply gap opening in 2-3 years that creates an entry window for new players."

Keep asking "so what" until you reach an actionable implication. If you can't get there, the data point may not belong in the synthesis.

### 3. Recommendations Under Uncertainty

Market analysis rarely produces certainty. Structure recommendations to acknowledge this honestly:

**State your confidence level:**
- **High confidence** -- multiple independent data sources confirm, structural logic is clear, low sensitivity to assumptions.
- **Medium confidence** -- directionally supported but depends on 1-2 key assumptions that could shift.
- **Low confidence** -- plausible interpretation but limited data, high sensitivity to assumptions.

**Use scenario framing for medium/low confidence recommendations:**
- "If [assumption A] holds, then [recommendation X]."
- "If [assumption A] fails, then [recommendation Y]."
- Identify the signposts that would tell you which scenario is unfolding.

**Separate the reversible from the irreversible:**
- For reversible decisions (pricing, marketing channel, hiring pace): recommend acting now and adjusting based on data. Speed matters more than precision.
- For irreversible decisions (market entry, major acquisition, platform choice): recommend investing in resolving key uncertainties before committing. Identify the cheapest way to reduce uncertainty (pilot, customer interviews, small bet).

### 4. Presenting Trade-offs

Never present a single recommendation without its trade-offs. Decision-makers distrust analysis that has no downsides. Structure as:

- **Option A** -- [description]. Upside: [X]. Risk: [Y]. Best if: [condition].
- **Option B** -- [description]. Upside: [X]. Risk: [Y]. Best if: [condition].
- **Recommended: Option [A/B]** because [reasoning], with the key risk being [specific risk] which can be mitigated by [mitigation].

### 5. What Would Change My Mind

Every recommendation should include a "pre-mortem" section:
- What assumptions, if proven wrong, would invalidate this recommendation?
- What data points should be monitored as early warning signals?
- What is the timeline for reassessing?

This is not a weakness -- it is intellectual honesty that builds trust and creates a built-in review mechanism.

### 6. Anti-Patterns to Avoid

- **Data dump without synthesis** -- presenting 20 charts with no interpretation. Analysis without a point of view is just data processing.
- **False precision** -- stating "the market will be $14.3B by 2028" when the inputs are rough estimates. Use ranges.
- **Buried lead** -- putting the most important finding on page 15. Lead with the conclusion.
- **Consensus disguised as insight** -- restating what everyone already knows. If your analysis confirms conventional wisdom, say so explicitly and explain whether the consensus is correct or complacent.
- **Recommendation without action specificity** -- "the company should innovate more" is not a recommendation. "Allocate $2M to pilot [specific initiative] in Q3 with a go/no-go decision by Q4 based on [specific metric]" is.

## Application

When turning analysis into recommendations:
1. Write the headline finding first. If you can't state it in one sentence, your thinking isn't clear enough yet.
2. Build the supporting arguments (3-5 independent points).
3. Apply the so-what chain to every data point to ensure it connects to an actionable implication.
4. Frame recommendations with explicit confidence levels and scenario logic where appropriate.
5. Present trade-offs for any significant recommendation.
6. Include the "what would change my mind" section.
7. Structure the deliverable top-down: conclusion first, evidence last.

The goal is not to be right with certainty -- it is to be clear about what you believe, why you believe it, and what would change your mind.
