# Competitive Landscape Mapping

**Tags:** market-analysis, competitive-intelligence, strategy, positioning

## Core Principle
Map the competitive landscape as a structured system of strategic groups, positioning axes, and capability gaps -- not a flat list of rivals.

## Technique

### 1. Identify the Full Competitor Set
Start broad, then categorize:
- **Direct competitors** -- same product, same customer, same job-to-be-done.
- **Indirect competitors** -- different product, same job-to-be-done (substitutes).
- **Potential entrants** -- adjacent players with the capabilities or incentives to enter (upstream suppliers, downstream customers, companies in related markets).

Do not stop at the obvious names. The most dangerous competitors are often indirect or potential entrants that incumbents ignore.

### 2. Define Positioning Axes
Choose 2-3 axes that capture the dimensions customers actually use to make decisions. Common axes:
- Price vs. quality/premium
- Breadth vs. depth of offering
- Self-serve vs. high-touch
- SMB vs. enterprise focus
- Geographic scope (local vs. global)

Bad axes: anything that doesn't differentiate (e.g., "customer-centric" -- everyone claims this). The axes should produce a map where competitors cluster into distinct groups, not a single blob.

### 3. Build Strategic Groups
Plot competitors on the axes. Clusters that emerge are strategic groups -- companies competing with similar strategies. Key observations:
- **Mobility barriers** between groups -- what stops a low-end player from moving upmarket (or vice versa)?
- **White space** -- unoccupied regions of the map. Ask: is the white space empty because it's unattractive, or because nobody has figured it out yet?
- **Convergence vectors** -- which groups are moving toward each other?

### 4. Assess Each Competitor
For each meaningful competitor, capture:
- **Strategy** -- how they compete (cost leadership, differentiation, niche focus).
- **Strengths** -- what they do well and why (be specific: "strong distribution through 10K retail partners" not "good distribution").
- **Weaknesses** -- where they underperform and the structural reason.
- **Trajectory** -- are they gaining or losing momentum? Recent moves, funding, hires, product launches.
- **Likely next moves** -- given their strategy and trajectory, what will they do in the next 6-18 months?

### 5. Identify Structural Dynamics
Step back from individual competitors and assess:
- **Concentration** -- is the market consolidating or fragmenting?
- **Switching costs** -- how locked-in are customers?
- **Network effects** -- does any player benefit from scale in a compounding way?
- **Rate of disruption** -- how quickly are new entrants changing the game?

## Application

When asked to analyze a competitive landscape:
1. Start by defining the market boundary and the job-to-be-done. If the boundary is ambiguous, state your assumption.
2. Build the competitor set using the three-tier model (direct, indirect, potential).
3. Choose positioning axes and map strategic groups.
4. Go deep on the 3-5 most relevant competitors (the ones that most directly threaten or inform the client's strategy).
5. Conclude with structural dynamics and the key question: where is this landscape heading in 12-24 months?

Avoid the trap of treating competitive analysis as a static snapshot. Always include trajectory and likely moves. A competitor's current position matters less than where they are going.
