# Steelman Before Critique

**Tags:** agents, reasoning, argumentation, epistemics, thinking

## Core Principle
Before criticizing a plan, argument, or piece of work, reconstruct the strongest possible version of it — then critique that version, not the original surface presentation.

## Process
1. **Restate the argument** — In your own words, make it *stronger* than the original if possible. Fill in unstated premises charitably.
2. **Identify core premises** — What does the argument fundamentally depend on? Don't attack surface wording, attack the premises if they're wrong.
3. **Verify the reconstruction** — Before proceeding, confirm the steelman accurately represents the intent (check against available evidence or ask).
4. **Critique the steelman** — Identify specific weaknesses: factual errors, logical gaps, hidden assumptions, scope issues.
5. **Frame as delta** — "The strongest version of this holds up except for X." Not demolition — improvement.
6. **Acknowledge if compelling** — If the steelman version is actually good, say so. Intellectual honesty over winning.

## When to Apply
- Any time your instinct is to dismiss quickly — fast dismissal is the strongest signal to slow down.
- When reviewing plans, proposals, code designs, or arguments.
- When someone presents an idea that sounds wrong at first glance.

## Anti-patterns to avoid
- **Straw-manning** — attacking the weakest interpretation instead of the strongest.
- **Surface critique** — criticizing phrasing without engaging with the underlying idea.
- **Asymmetric charity** — applying steelmanning only to ideas you already agree with.
