# Evidence-Graded Research Synthesis

**Tags:** agents, research, synthesis, epistemics, writing

## Core Principle
When synthesizing information across multiple sources, explicitly grade each claim by evidence quality and flag gaps — never flatten strong and weak evidence into the same confident voice.

## Evidence Hierarchy (strongest to weakest)
1. Replicated primary research (multiple independent studies)
2. Single primary research paper
3. Systematic review / meta-analysis
4. Expert opinion from domain practitioner
5. Journalistic summary / secondary source
6. Anecdote / single case

## Process
1. **Collect and classify sources** — For each key claim, note: (a) number of independent sources, (b) methodological quality, (c) recency, (d) potential conflicts of interest.
2. **Grade confidence explicitly** — What is known (replicated, high-confidence) vs. probable (limited evidence) vs. speculative.
3. **Surface contradictions** — "Source A says X, Source B says Y; the discrepancy may be due to Z" — never silently average conflicting evidence.
4. **Identify gaps** — What question do the sources *not* answer that the task requires? Name it explicitly.
5. **Write calibrated conclusions** — Use: "strong evidence suggests," "preliminary evidence indicates," "it is speculated that" — matching language to confidence level.

## Rules
- The confidence of a chain of inferences is limited by the least-supported step.
- Surprising, precise, or consequential claims require stronger sourcing than background context.
- A chain of secondary sources does not add up to a primary source.
- If you can't find a primary source, say so — "widely cited but source unverified" rather than presenting as fact.
