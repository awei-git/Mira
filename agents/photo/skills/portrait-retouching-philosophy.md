# Portrait Retouching Philosophy

**Tags:** photo, editing, retouching, portrait, skin

## Core Principle
Retouch portraits to enhance rather than erase — preserve identity, texture, and specificity while removing only distractions. The goal is invisible craft: the viewer sees the subject, not the retoucher's decisions.

## Temporary vs. Permanent Rule
The most important retouching distinction:
- **Remove**: active blemishes, stray hairs over the face, lint on clothing, sensor dust
- **Preserve**: freckles, birthmarks, scars, natural wrinkles, asymmetry, pores

Ask: "Would the subject recognize this as themselves in ten years?" If not, undo.

## Frequency Separation
The standard technique for separating texture from tone:
- **Low-frequency layer**: color and tone only (Gaussian blur, radius 3–8px). Smooth uneven skin tones and color casts here.
- **High-frequency layer**: texture only (pores, fine hairs). Remove specific blemishes here without affecting surrounding skin tone.
- Result: smoothing never destroys texture — skin looks smooth but not plastic.

## Dodge and Burn for Sculpting
Retouch with light, not paint:
- Two empty layers: one Dodge (Screen blend), one Burn (Multiply), both filled with 50% gray.
- Low-opacity white brush (2–6%) on Dodge layer: lift shadows, flatten minor irregularities.
- Low-opacity black brush on Burn layer: add definition at nasolabial folds, jawline, eyelashes.
- This mimics what better lighting would have achieved in camera.

## Eyes
- Preserve all catchlights — never clone or smooth them.
- Sharpen the iris slightly (High Pass 1–2px, Soft Light blend, masked to iris only).
- Maintain microcontrast in skin — if texture looks uniform, you have gone too far.

## Verification Protocol
1. 1:1 zoom: check for cloning artifacts and texture irregularities
2. Fit-to-screen: plastic-looking edits visible at 100% become obvious here
3. Before/after blink: toggle visibility rapidly. Different person's face = undo.
4. Grayscale check: desaturate temporarily to catch dodge/burn color casts
