# Decisive Moment Selection

**Tags:** photo, editing, selection, street, curation

## Core Principle
Apply Cartier-Bresson's contact-sheet discipline to editing — select the one frame where form, gesture, and meaning coincide simultaneously, not the technically best frame.

## The Selection Process
1. **Contact sheet** — Lay out the full sequence before evaluating any single frame (Lightroom Survey View).
2. **Technical cut** — Eliminate on focus, motion blur, clipping. Fast and mechanical.
3. **Expressive cut** — Select on gesture, geometry, and meaning. Slow and deliberate.
4. **Blink test** — Does the selected frame work without any caption or context?
5. **Process minimal** — Tonal/contrast adjustments only. The moment must carry itself.

## Timing: The Beat Before and After
The "peak action" frame is often NOT the decisive moment. Check:
- One frame before: anticipation
- The moment: maximum action
- Two frames after: consequence

Cartier-Bresson's jumping puddle image is from the beat before landing — body extended, reflection aligned. One frame later the geometry collapses.

## Geometric Alignment Test
The decisive moment has two components: **content** (what is happening) and **form** (how elements compose at that moment). Ask: do the moving elements align into a latent geometric structure (diagonals, circles, triangles)?

If two frames have equal expressive content, select the one with superior compositional alignment.

## Minimal Processing for Street Work
The authority of street photography comes from rawness. Over-processing defeats this.
- **Permitted**: exposure correction, contrast, highlight/shadow recovery, crop (sparingly), B&W conversion
- **Never**: heavy local adjustments, sky replacement, content-aware fill to remove elements

## The Caption-Free Test
After selection: would this image communicate its essential meaning to a viewer with no caption, title, or context? If not, the image has not been fully realized.
