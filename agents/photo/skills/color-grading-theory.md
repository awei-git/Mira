# Color Grading Theory

**Tags:** photo, editing, color, grading, mood

## One-liner
Color is emotion made visible — deliberate control of hue, saturation, and temperature transforms a photograph from a record of light into a feeling.

## The Problem
Ungraded photos often feel "correct" but emotionally flat. The camera captures color as-is, but human memory and emotion encode color differently. A sunset remembered is always warmer than a sunset photographed. Color grading bridges that gap — it makes the image feel the way the moment felt. Done poorly, grading looks like a filter slapped on. Done well, it is invisible and inevitable.

## The Pattern

```
1. CORRECT  — Fix white balance first. Start from neutral before pushing toward a mood.
2. INTENT   — Decide the emotional target: warm nostalgia, cold isolation, vibrant energy, muted calm.
3. PALETTE  — Choose a color relationship: complementary, analogous, monochromatic, or split-complementary.
4. GRADE    — Shift shadows, midtones, and highlights independently toward your target colors.
5. UNIFY    — Ensure all elements in the frame feel like they belong to the same color world.
```

## Techniques

**1. Color harmony systems**
These are relationships on the color wheel that naturally feel balanced:
- **Complementary**: Two colors opposite each other (teal and orange, blue and yellow, purple and green). High contrast, high energy. The most common cinematic grade — teal shadows with orange skin tones — works because it is complementary.
- **Analogous**: Three colors adjacent on the wheel (blue, teal, green or red, orange, yellow). Low contrast, cohesive, calm. Autumn foliage is naturally analogous. So is a blue-hour cityscape.
- **Monochromatic**: One hue at varying saturations and brightnesses. Powerful for mood. A sepia-toned image is monochromatic. So is a desaturated blue-grey rain scene.
- **Split-complementary**: One base color plus the two colors adjacent to its complement. Provides contrast with more nuance than pure complementary. Less common but sophisticated.

**2. Color temperature as emotion**
Temperature is the most direct emotional lever in color grading:
- **Warm (amber/orange shift)**: Comfort, nostalgia, intimacy, golden hour, memory. Push white balance toward warm or add orange to midtones.
- **Cool (blue shift)**: Distance, isolation, clinical precision, night, melancholy. Push white balance toward cool or add blue to shadows.
- **Neutral**: Documentary honesty, modern, clean. Sometimes the most powerful choice is restraint.
- **Mixed warm/cool**: Warm highlights with cool shadows (or vice versa) creates visual depth and separation. The subject (warm) appears to come forward from the background (cool).

**3. Split toning: shadows, midtones, highlights**
The most powerful grading technique. Instead of shifting all colors uniformly, shift different tonal ranges toward different hues:
- **Shadows toward teal + highlights toward warm orange**: The classic cinematic look. Creates depth because cool recedes and warm advances.
- **Shadows toward deep blue + highlights toward pale yellow**: Moonlight feel. Quiet and contemplative.
- **Shadows toward purple + highlights toward gold**: Rich and luxurious. Works for fashion and portrait.
- The key: subtlety. A 10-15% shift is usually enough. If the viewer consciously notices the color grading, it is probably too heavy.

**4. Saturation control**
Saturation is volume — how loud the colors shout:
- **Global desaturation**: Muted colors feel vintage, serious, or editorial. Pull saturation 20-40% for a "film" look.
- **Selective saturation**: Desaturate everything except one color range. A figure in a red coat against a grey city — the selective saturation is the story.
- **Vibrance vs. saturation**: Vibrance boosts muted colors while protecting already-saturated colors and skin tones. Saturation boosts everything equally. For portraits, vibrance is almost always the better tool.
- **Oversaturation warning**: Saturated photos grab attention on a phone screen but fatigue the eye quickly. If skin looks orange or skies look electric, pull back.

**5. Color grading for consistency**
A series of photos (a set, a story, a feed) must share a color world:
- Develop a grade on one hero image, then apply it to the rest and fine-tune.
- Anchor on 2-3 dominant colors across the series. Let those recur.
- Consistency does not mean identical — it means the viewer feels that all images belong to the same universe.

## When to Use
- Every photo that will be shared or published. Raw files are unfinished — they are ingredients, not meals.
- Especially critical in: portrait work (skin tone accuracy within a mood), landscape (golden hour warmth vs. blue hour cool), editorial/commercial (brand color consistency), storytelling series (visual continuity across images).

## Common Pitfalls
- **Grading before correcting**: If white balance is wrong, every color shift builds on a broken foundation. Correct first, then grade.
- **Skin tone destruction**: Human skin has a narrow acceptable color range. When grading, always check skin tones — they should never go green, grey, or neon orange.
- **Trend chasing**: The orange-and-teal look, the faded-blacks look, the pastel look — these are tools, not identities. Choose a grade because it serves the image, not because it is popular.
- **Inconsistent series**: Five photos from the same shoot graded five different ways looks like indecision. Commit to a palette.
- **Crushing the blacks for "mood"**: Lifting the black point (faded blacks) works for certain aesthetics. But doing it on every image removes tonal depth. True blacks give an image weight and grounding.
