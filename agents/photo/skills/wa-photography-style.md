# WA Photography Style — Learned from 1162 Photos

**Tags:** photo, style, personal, editing, composition, selection

## One-liner
Cinematic landscape photographer who excels at light-chasing, warm-cool color separation, and compositional depth — strongest when patient, weakest when rushing snapshots.

## Style DNA (from 1162 reviewed photos, IG + LRed)

### What makes a WA photo great (patterns from 63 photos scoring 7.5+)

**1. Light is the subject, not the location.**
The top-scoring images aren't the most famous locations — they're the best light. Raking sidelight on sand dunes (8.3), blue-hour warm/cool contrast at a gazebo (8.4), golden backlight through autumn branches (7.9). The common thread: WA waited for or recognized a specific light condition that transforms the scene. When the light is ordinary, the score drops regardless of how spectacular the location is.

**2. Warm-cool complementary color palette.**
The signature look: amber/orange warm tones against teal/blue cool tones. This appears naturally in golden hour, autumn foliage against blue sky, lit interiors against dusk. In post-processing: orange hue shift -22, yellow -30, red saturation killed (-73), orange/yellow/green boosted, split toning with amber shadows + gold highlights. The grade is cinematic but rooted in real light — it enhances what's there rather than manufacturing what isn't.

**3. Compositional depth through layers.**
Best shots consistently have 3+ depth layers: foreground interest (footprints, moss, rock), midground subject (dunes, gazebo, cliff), background atmosphere (sky, fog, reflection). The weakest shots lack foreground or have a single flat plane. Leading lines (footprints, branches, waterways) are a reliable compositional tool.

**4. Human scale for narrative.**
Small human figures in vast landscapes — kayakers on a lake, a lone figure atop a dune. Creates story and scale simultaneously. Not portraits of people, but portraits of human smallness in nature.

**5. Patience for rare convergence.**
The highest-scoring shots capture moments where multiple conditions align: autumn + first snow + blue hour + glass-still water (8.1). These aren't lucky — they require being present and recognizing the moment.

### What makes a WA photo weak (patterns from 78 photos scoring ≤5.0)

**1. Bad light, especially harsh midday.**
The single most consistent failure. Overhead sun with no modeling, no catch lights, no directional quality. Every bottom-10 photo has flat or harsh light. The solution isn't post-processing — it's waiting or not shooting.

**2. Background chaos in people shots.**
Traffic cones, signs, buildings, crowds competing with the subject. No depth-of-field separation. Background management is the #1 gap between top and bottom (4.3 points difference).

**3. Snapshot mode — no compositional intention.**
Centered horizon, no foreground anchor, subject too small or too far, no leading lines. The camera was pointed at something interesting but the frame wasn't composed.

**4. Faces hidden or too far.**
Portraits where eyes are invisible (masks, hats, distance). A portrait that can't show expression isn't a portrait.

**5. No post-processing applied.**
Several low-scoring images are essentially straight-out-of-camera with flat, grey, undeveloped RAW appearance. The cinematic grade is a significant part of the WA look — without it, the images feel unfinished.

## Stats
- IG average: 7.0/10 (95 photos) — good selection instinct
- LRed average: 6.2/10 (1066 photos) — includes all attempts
- Best genre: landscape/nature (avg 6.5) > portrait/people (avg 5.9)
- Best period: late 2020 (desert + winter trips) and late 2024 (Mohonk autumn)
- Weakest: Disney/family snapshots (5.0), casual gatherings (4.9)

## Rules for the Photo Agent

1. **Never shoot in flat midday light** — if reviewing a batch, flag and deprioritize midday shots.
2. **When selecting from a set, rank by light quality first** — the best light wins even if composition is imperfect.
3. **For landscape edits, always apply the warm-cool separation** — this is the WA signature.
4. **Require 3 depth layers for landscape picks** — foreground, subject, background. Reject single-plane compositions.
5. **For people shots, verify: eyes visible, background clean, light directional** — if any fails, flag it.
6. **Every RAW gets the grade** — no unprocessed exports. The cinematic toning is not optional.
7. **When in doubt, favor atmosphere over technical perfection** — a moody 7 beats a sharp 5.
