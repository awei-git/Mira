# Composition and Framing

**Tags:** photo, editing, composition, framing

## One-liner
Guide the viewer's eye through deliberate placement of elements — where things sit in the frame determines what the photo says.

## The Problem
Snapshots capture subjects. Photographs compose them. The difference is intentional placement. A centered subject with no spatial logic feels flat. A cluttered frame with no hierarchy feels chaotic. Composition is how you turn a rectangle into a statement.

## The Pattern

```
1. SUBJECT  — Decide what the photo is about. One primary subject, always.
2. PLACE    — Position the subject using a structural grid (thirds, golden ratio, center).
3. DIRECT   — Use lines, shapes, and contrast to pull the eye toward the subject.
4. BALANCE  — Fill or empty the remaining space with intention.
5. EXCLUDE  — Remove anything that doesn't serve the composition. Crop ruthlessly.
```

## Techniques

**1. Rule of thirds**
Divide the frame into a 3x3 grid. Place the subject at an intersection point, not the center. Horizons go on the top or bottom third line, never the middle. This creates natural visual tension — the eye moves through the frame instead of landing and stopping.
- Portrait: eyes on the upper-third line.
- Landscape: horizon on the lower third (emphasize sky) or upper third (emphasize ground).
- When to break it: symmetrical subjects (reflections, architecture) can demand dead center. Break the rule on purpose, never by accident.

**2. Leading lines**
Any line in the frame that points toward the subject — roads, fences, shadows, arms, rivers, architectural edges. The eye follows lines involuntarily. Diagonal lines create energy. Horizontal lines create calm. Vertical lines create strength. Converging lines create depth.
- Strong: a path curving from the bottom corner toward a figure in the upper third.
- Weak: lines that lead to nothing or lead the eye out of the frame.

**3. Negative space**
Empty space around the subject is not wasted — it is emphasis. The more space around a subject, the more isolated and significant it feels. Negative space creates breathing room, elegance, and scale.
- A lone figure against a vast sky: the emptiness IS the story.
- A product on a clean background: the space says "focus here."
- Too little negative space makes the frame feel cramped and the subject feel trapped.

**4. Visual hierarchy**
The viewer's eye should move through the frame in a deliberate order: primary subject first, secondary elements second, background last. Control hierarchy through:
- **Size**: Larger elements draw attention first.
- **Contrast**: High-contrast areas pull the eye before low-contrast areas.
- **Sharpness**: Sharp subjects dominate over blurred backgrounds (depth of field as compositional tool).
- **Color**: Saturated or warm colors advance; muted or cool colors recede.

**5. Framing within frames**
Use elements in the scene — doorways, windows, arches, branches, shadows — to create a frame around the subject. This adds depth, context, and directs attention inward. The inner frame doesn't need to be complete; even a partial border works.

## When to Use
- Every photo. Composition is not a style choice — it is the foundational grammar of photography. A technically perfect image with poor composition says nothing.
- Especially critical in: street photography (fast decisions, no second chances), portrait work (eye placement and head room), landscape (horizon and foreground relationships), product photography (negative space and hierarchy).

## Common Pitfalls
- **Centering everything**: Center composition works for symmetry. For everything else, it produces static, lifeless images.
- **Tilted horizons**: Unless the Dutch angle is intentional and dramatic, a horizon off by 1-2 degrees looks like a mistake. Always check.
- **Edge collisions**: Subjects touching or barely clipping the frame edge create visual tension in the wrong way. Either give clear space or crop boldly into the subject.
- **Ignoring the background**: A strong subject against a cluttered or distracting background loses its power. Simplify the background or use depth of field to suppress it.
- **Over-composing**: Not every photo needs leading lines AND rule of thirds AND framing. One strong compositional idea is better than three competing ones.
