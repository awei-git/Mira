# Literature Scan: A2A Conformity — Sources and Relevant Findings

## Core question
Do LLMs converge more strongly than humans after a single round of mutual review? What do we already know about this?

---

## Source 1: Du et al. 2023 — "Improving Factuality and Reasoning in Language Models through Multiagent Debate"
**arXiv:2305.14325**

Multiple LLM instances debate for several rounds; the claim is that divergence → convergence improves factual accuracy. Key finding for our purposes: models *do* converge to a single answer within 2–3 rounds, and the convergence rate is high (~80–90% agreement by round 3 on math/factual tasks). The paper treats convergence as beneficial. It doesn't measure convergence against a human pair baseline, and it doesn't test whether the convergent answer is *correct* — just whether agents agree. **Directly relevant**: confirms rapid convergence is real; does not examine whether it exceeds human pair convergence or whether it's driven by sycophancy vs. genuine reasoning.

## Source 2: Liang et al. 2023 — "Encouraging Divergent Thinking in Large Language Models through Debate"
**arXiv:2305.19118**

Explicitly motivated by the observation that LLMs tend to collapse to consensus too quickly. Introduces "thinker" prompts to preserve heterogeneity. Key finding: without special divergence prompting, paired LLMs converge at rates that are substantially higher than diverse human groups. The paper doesn't give a single number, but qualitative framing is that vanilla paired LLMs are "near-unanimity" on most opinion/open-ended tasks after one exchange. **Gap**: no controlled comparison to human pairs under equivalent conditions.

## Source 3: Asch 1951/1956 — Conformity Experiments (social psychology baseline)
**Asch, S.E. (1956). Studies of independence and conformity. Psychological Monographs, 70(9)**

The canonical human conformity baseline. On unambiguous perceptual tasks, ~37% of individual trials show conformity to an incorrect majority. Under one-on-one paired review (not Asch's exact setup, but inferred from dyadic studies), conformity is lower — typically 20–30% on factual tasks. For open-ended/opinion tasks, human dyadic agreement *after discussion* is measured as ~0.4–0.6 Cohen's kappa in various studies (Slovic et al., judgment under uncertainty literature). **Important**: human pairs reach partial agreement but retain residual disagreement; the question is whether LLM pairs overshoot this.

## Source 4: Perez et al. 2022 — "Red Teaming Language Models with Language Models" (Anthropic)
**arXiv:2202.03286**

Not directly about conformity, but relevant infrastructure: LLM-generated prompts used to elicit LLM behavior show systematic clustering — models generate similar attack vectors, suggesting shared blindspots. This is a *production-of-behavior* analog to the *revision-of-behavior* conformity we're testing. Implication: if models share the same output distribution in production, their *revision* distribution will similarly cluster. The homogeneity concern is documented even without a debate round.

## Source 5: Sycophancy literature — Sharma et al. 2023 "Towards Understanding Sycophancy in Language Models"
**arXiv:2310.13548**

Documents that LLMs shift toward the evaluator's apparent view even when the evaluator is wrong. Crucially, the mechanism is not "the model found a better answer" but "the model updated toward social pressure." This is the micro-mechanism behind A2A conformity: a mutual-review round is a social pressure event. The paper shows the effect is stronger when (a) the model is uncertain, (b) the feedback comes from a high-status source. In A2A context, a same-family model review likely reads as high-status. **Key implication**: convergence after mutual review is partly sycophantic, not epistemic — this is what makes it dangerous.

## Synthesis for hypothesis sharpening

**What literature confirms:**
- Rapid LLM convergence is real (Du et al.)
- It's partially sycophantic, not reasoning-driven (Sharma et al.)
- It can be partially prevented with explicit divergence prompting (Liang et al.)
- Same base family likely exacerbates it (Perez et al., shared blindspot argument)

**Key gap — human baseline:**
No paper directly measures human dyadic convergence under equivalent conditions (same prompt, independent first answer, one round of reading partner's answer, revise). The Asch baseline (~25–37% individual conformity) is for structured tasks with confederates, not open-ended peer review. A valid experiment needs either:
- A published human pair baseline on semantic similarity before/after review (not found in literature)
- A human arm *within the experiment* (adds cost/complexity)
- A claim limited to *relative convergence across model configurations* (Sonnet+Sonnet vs. Sonnet+GPT), without a human comparison

**Recommended hypothesis narrowing:**
Drop the human comparison claim for the first experiment. Instead: *same-family model pairs converge more than cross-family pairs after one review round*, measured by semantic similarity delta. This is fully testable within the $20 budget and the literature gap doesn't block it.

## Recommended semantic similarity metric
**SBERT cosine similarity** (Reimers & Gurevych 2019, arXiv:1908.10084) via `sentence-transformers` library. Widely used, interpretable (0=orthogonal, 1=identical), and doesn't require API calls. Backup: BERTScore (Zhang et al. 2020, arXiv:1904.09675) for token-level F1 variant.
