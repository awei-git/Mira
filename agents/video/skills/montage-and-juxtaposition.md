# Montage and Juxtaposition

**Tags:** video, editing, montage, meaning

## Core Principle
Meaning emerges from the collision of two shots — what you place next to each other creates ideas that neither shot contains alone.

## Technique
The Kuleshov effect: the same shot of a face means different things depending on what follows it (food = hunger, coffin = grief, child = tenderness). Editing doesn't just assemble shots — it creates meaning.

**Types of montage:**
1. **Metric** — Cuts at fixed intervals regardless of content. Creates mechanical, driving rhythm. (Music videos, action sequences)
2. **Rhythmic** — Cut timing follows the visual rhythm of the content (motion, gesture). More organic than metric.
3. **Tonal** — Shots selected for emotional quality (light, texture, pace). Builds mood through accumulation.
4. **Intellectual** — Juxtapose contrasting images to create an argument. (Wealth next to poverty, war next to play)
5. **Associative** — Connect thematically related images across space/time. The viewer's mind draws the connection.

**Practical juxtaposition patterns:**
- Contrast: show opposites to highlight each (silence then explosion, stillness then chaos)
- Parallel: intercut two related actions to suggest connection
- Cause-effect: shot A leads to shot B as consequence
- Question-answer: shot A poses implicit question, shot B answers

## Application
- Before assembling a sequence, ask: "What idea should emerge from these shots together?"
- Test: cover one shot — does the sequence still communicate the idea? If yes, the juxtaposition isn't doing work
- Use intellectual montage sparingly — it can feel heavy-handed. Tonal and associative are more subtle
