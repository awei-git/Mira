# Documentary Assembly Edit

**Tags:** video, editing, documentary, structure, storytelling

## Core Principle
Build documentary structure from the ground up — transcribe and paper-cut first, assemble from story logic rather than chronology, and find the film inside the footage before touching the timeline.

## The Three-Phase Process

**Phase 1 — Ingestion and Transcription**
- Log all footage: label every clip with content, quality (A/B/C), and emotional value (not just technical value).
- Transcribe all interviews and sync audio — the paper cut lives in the transcripts, not in the timeline.
- Build a "selects bin" of the best material; do not edit from the full footage bin.

**Phase 2 — Paper Cut**
- Work entirely in the transcript: mark the best lines, cut what is redundant, rearrange for story logic.
- Find the spine: what is the film actually about (theme), and what is the narrative arc (story)? These are different questions.
- The paper cut should run 20–40% longer than the target runtime — cutting is easier than filling.
- Key question for every line: does this advance the spine, deepen character, or provide essential context? If none of the three, cut it.

**Phase 3 — Assembly and Rough Cut**
- Assemble from the paper cut, not from chronology. Chronology is the enemy of story.
- Build on interview/narration as the primary structural layer; B-roll, archive, and music are secondary layers added after.
- First assembly will be rough and long — this is normal. The rough cut reveals structural problems; solve them structurally before any fine-cutting.

## The Structural Arc (for character-driven documentaries)
1. **Status quo**: who is this person, what is their world?
2. **Inciting disruption**: what changes or challenges that world?
3. **Journey and obstacle**: what does the protagonist do, and what resists them?
4. **Transformation or revelation**: what changes as a result?
5. **New reality**: where do they end up?

## Rules
- Never fine-cut before the structure is locked. Polish is wasted on scenes that will be removed.
- Watch the rough cut with fresh eyes (after a break) — the film you made is not always the film you intended.
