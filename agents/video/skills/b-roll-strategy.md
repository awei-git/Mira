# B-Roll Strategy

**Tags:** video, editing, b-roll, storytelling, visual-coverage

## Core Principle
B-roll is not decoration — it is a parallel narrative layer that either illustrates, contrasts, or extends the meaning of the primary audio track. Purposeless B-roll makes edits feel random; purposeful B-roll makes them feel inevitable.

## The Four Functions of B-Roll
1. **Illustrative**: shows what the speaker is describing ("We walked through the market" → market footage)
2. **Contrastive**: creates irony or tension by showing something that contradicts the audio
3. **Atmospheric**: establishes location, time of day, mood — expository but necessary
4. **Bridging**: covers a jump cut or interview edit while maintaining audio continuity

## B-Roll Hierarchy
Not all B-roll is equal. In order of narrative value:
- **Specific and irreplaceable**: footage that could only have been captured in this story (the exact location, the specific person, the actual event)
- **Illustrative but generic**: could illustrate many stories in this genre (hands on a keyboard, city streets)
- **Filler**: used only to cover an edit — avoid if possible; reveals a structural problem

Maximize specific footage; minimize filler.

## Coverage Planning (pre-production mindset)
- Before shooting, list every statement, concept, or emotion in the interview that will need visual support.
- Plan B-roll shots systematically: wide (context), medium (activity), tight (detail). Each scene needs all three.
- Capture process shots: the subject doing something, not just standing. Action B-roll cuts more fluidly than static.

## In the Edit
- Lay the full audio track first, then add B-roll — not the reverse. B-roll serves audio, not vice versa.
- Never cut B-roll in a way that contradicts the audio's timeline (showing "arrival" footage over "departure" narration).
- B-roll duration: cut out before the viewer has fully processed the image (leaves the viewer wanting) rather than holding until they're bored.
- J-cuts and L-cuts: audio leads the cut in and out, making transitions feel organic rather than mechanical.
