# Sentence Rhythm (句子节奏)

## One-liner
Control the pace, emphasis, and emotional texture of prose through deliberate variation in sentence length, structure, and sound — prose as music.

## The Problem
Uniform sentence length creates monotony. Five medium-length sentences in a row, each with the same structure, each ending with similar cadence — the reader's ear goes numb. The words are fine individually. The rhythm kills them collectively. This is the most common prose flaw that writers don't notice because they read for content, not for sound.

## The Pattern

```
1. WRITE the passage for content first
2. READ IT ALOUD — listen for monotony, rhythm, where your voice
   naturally rises and falls
3. VARY: alternate long flowing sentences with short punches
4. MATCH rhythm to emotion: short for impact, long for immersion
5. USE fragments and parallel structure as deliberate tools
```

## Techniques

**1. The punch pattern**
A series of longer sentences followed by a short one. The short sentence hits like a hammer.

"She had spent the whole afternoon preparing the house, setting out the good china, arranging flowers she'd cut from the garden that morning, placing the wine to breathe on the counter where he'd see it first."
"He didn't come home."

(The long sentence builds. The short sentence destroys.)

**2. Fragments for intensity**
Not grammatically correct. Emotionally correct.

"The phone rang. She grabbed it. Nothing. Static. Then breathing."

(The mind under pressure doesn't speak in complete sentences. Fragments replicate cognitive fragmentation.)

**3. Long sentences for immersion**
Use when you want the reader to lose themselves in a flow of sensation, memory, or movement.

"He walked through the market where the air smelled of cumin and charcoal and the stalls were draped in fabric that caught the light and turned it colors he didn't have words for, orange that was almost red, blue that was almost black, and somewhere a radio was playing a song he'd heard before in another country, another life."

(The sentence itself IS the experience of being immersed.)

**4. Parallel structure for emphasis**
"She wanted order. She wanted silence. She wanted him gone."
(The repetition creates a drumbeat. Each repetition raises the stakes.)

**5. The interrupted sentence**
A long sentence that breaks itself mid-thought — with a dash, a period, a sudden turn.

"He was going to explain everything, the money, the phone calls, the reason he'd been coming home late for — but she was already packing."

(The interruption mirrors the character's plan being interrupted by reality.)

**6. Sentence length as pacing control**
Action scenes: short sentences. Each one is a beat.
Contemplative scenes: longer sentences. The reader settles in.
Transitions: medium sentences. Neutral rhythm.

The shift between modes should be gradual, not abrupt — unless you want the abruptness to be the effect.

## The Provost Principle
Gary Provost's demonstration:

"This sentence has five words. Here are five more words. Five-word sentences are fine. But several together become monotonous. Listen to what is happening. The writing is getting boring. The sound of it drones. It's like a stuck record. The ear demands some variety.

Now listen. I vary my sentence length, and I create music. Music. The writing sings. It has a pleasant rhythm, a lilt, a harmony."

## When to Use
- Every piece of prose benefits from rhythm awareness
- Action scenes (short, percussive)
- Emotional climaxes (the punch pattern)
- Lyrical passages (long, flowing)
- Revision — rhythm problems are best caught in revision, not first draft

## Common Pitfalls
- **All short sentences**: Reads like a telegram. Exhausting. No flow.
- **All long sentences**: Reads like a legal document. Reader loses the thread.
- **Rhythmic monotony disguised by varied content**: The sentences are about different things, but they all have the same length and structure. Content variety doesn't fix rhythm monotony.
- **Overusing fragments**: Fragments are spice. Too much spice kills the dish.
- **Ignoring sound**: Read aloud. If you stumble, the reader will too. If it sounds wrong, it is wrong.
