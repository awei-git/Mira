# Desire and Contradiction (欲望与矛盾)

## One-liner
Build characters around a core contradiction — what they want vs. what they need, or what they say vs. what they do — and let the story force the contradiction to the surface.

## The Problem
Flat characters want one thing and pursue it consistently. They're coherent, logical, and boring. Real people are bundles of contradictions: they want things that conflict with each other, say things that contradict what they do, and believe things that contradict what they need. Fiction that captures this contradiction feels alive. Fiction that doesn't, reads like a chess problem.

## The Pattern

```
1. GIVE the character a CONSCIOUS WANT (what they're actively pursuing)
2. GIVE them an UNCONSCIOUS NEED (what they actually require,
   which contradicts the want)
3. DESIGN situations that force the want and need into collision
4. REVEAL character through CHOICES UNDER PRESSURE —
   what they choose when they can't have both
5. The story's resolution comes when the character either
   integrates the contradiction or is destroyed by it
```

## Techniques

**1. Want vs. Need**
Conscious want: what the character pursues through action.
Unconscious need: what they require for genuine fulfillment, which they can't see or won't admit.
The gap between them IS the character arc.

Walter White wants to provide for his family (want). He needs to feel powerful and significant (need). The story puts these in collision until the need devours the want.

**2. Value contradiction**
The character holds two values that cannot coexist. The story forces a choice.
"I value loyalty" + "I value truth" = what happens when the truth would betray a friend?
The choice they make under this pressure reveals who they really are.

**3. Self-image vs. behavior**
The character believes they are one kind of person. Their actions reveal another.
Stevens in *Remains of the Day* believes he is a man of dignity and honor. His actions reveal a man who sacrificed love and moral responsibility for servility. He never sees the contradiction. The reader does.

**4. The "except when" test**
For each major character, complete both sentences:
"They would never ___."
"Except when ___."
If you can only complete the first: the character is too consistent (flat).
If you can only complete the second: the character is too random (incoherent).
Both completed: the character has a specific, interesting fault line.

**5. Contradictory desire as engine**
A character who wants two incompatible things generates their own conflict without needing an external antagonist.
"She wanted to be free. She wanted to be safe."
Every scene becomes about which desire she serves — and what it costs.

## When to Use
- Character creation (build contradiction in from the start)
- When a character feels "flat" or "too consistent"
- When a story lacks internal conflict (external antagonist isn't enough)
- Character arc design — the arc IS the process of confronting the contradiction

## Common Pitfalls
- **Random inconsistency**: A character who's brave in one scene and cowardly in the next for no reason isn't contradictory — they're broken. The contradiction must have an internal logic.
- **Contradiction as flaw only**: Contradiction can be a source of strength, not just weakness. A character's want and need can both be admirable — just incompatible.
- **Resolving too easily**: If the character simply "realizes" their need in chapter 3, there's no story. The contradiction should be resistant to easy resolution.
- **Forgetting the external**: Internal contradiction needs external situations that activate it. The contradiction is latent until the plot puts pressure on it.

## Source
