# Psychic Distance (心理距离光谱)

## One-liner
Control how close or far the reader feels from a character's consciousness using John Gardner's five-level spectrum — zoom in for emotional peaks, pull back for context.

## The Problem
Amateur fiction stays locked at one distance — usually a flat middle range that's neither intimate nor panoramic. The prose feels monotonous. The reader never gets fully immersed in a character's mind, and never gets the relief of a wide establishing shot. The result reads like a camera stuck at one focal length.

## The Pattern

```
1. IDENTIFY the emotional importance of each passage
2. MAP it to a distance level (1=farthest, 5=closest)
3. MOVE GRADUALLY between levels — never jump from 1 to 5
4. ZOOM IN for emotional climaxes (levels 4-5)
5. PULL BACK for transitions and establishing shots (levels 1-2)
6. VARY the distance within scenes for rhythm
```

## The Five Levels

**Level 1 — Panoramic (narrator is god)**
"It was winter of the year 1853. A large man stepped out of a doorway."
No interiority. Pure cinema. Useful for openings, time jumps, establishing context.

**Level 2 — Biographical (named but external)**
"Henry Warburton had never liked snowstorms."
Character is named, has opinions, but the narrator's voice dominates.

**Level 3 — Limited (character attitude leaks in)**
"Henry hated these damn snowstorms. He pulled his coat tighter."
The word "damn" is Henry's — the narrator wouldn't say it. The character's attitude infects the narration.

**Level 4 — Close (character's voice dominates)**
"God, how he hated these snowstorms. Every step was misery."
The sentence rhythm, exclamations, and word choice all belong to the character.

**Level 5 — Immersed (direct consciousness)**
"Snow. Everywhere, snow. Fingers numb. Toes — he couldn't feel his toes. Stop. Keep moving."
Fragments. Sensation. No narrator mediation. The reader IS inside the character's mind.

## Techniques

**1. The gradual zoom**
Start a chapter at level 1-2, gradually move to 4-5 as the scene intensifies.
"The restaurant was crowded that Friday evening." (Level 1)
"Maria found her usual table occupied." (Level 2)
"Of course it was occupied. The one night she needed to sit alone." (Level 3-4)
"The wine list. Focus on the wine list. Don't look at the door." (Level 5)

**2. The pull-back for impact**
After an intense level 5 passage, suddenly cut to level 1.
"She screamed into the pillow until her throat gave out." (Level 5)
"By morning, the snow had stopped." (Level 1)
(The distance itself produces the feeling of emotional exhaustion.)

**3. Distance mismatch for irony**
Describe a terrible situation at level 1-2.
"On Tuesday the fourteenth, approximately forty-three people drowned."
(The clinical distance IS the horror. The narrator's composure implies a reality too terrible for close engagement.)

## When to Use
- Every scene benefits from deliberate distance modulation
- Emotional climaxes: zoom to 4-5
- Opening paragraphs: typically 1-2, then zoom gradually
- After high-intensity passages: pull back to give the reader breathing room
- Comic or ironic passages: mismatch between content severity and distance level

## Common Pitfalls
- **Jumping levels**: Moving from level 1 to level 5 in one sentence is jarring. Transition through intermediate levels.
- **Stuck at level 3**: The safest distance is also the flattest. It's the "competent but boring" default.
- **All level 5**: Unrelenting intimacy is exhausting. Even in deep POV, the reader needs occasional level 2-3 relief.
- **Confusing distance with quality**: Close isn't "better" than far. Each level serves a purpose. The skill is in the movement between them.

## Source
John Gardner, *The Art of Fiction* (1983), Chapter 4. Now a standard framework in MFA programs. Complemented by James Wood's analysis of free indirect discourse in *How Fiction Works* (2008).
