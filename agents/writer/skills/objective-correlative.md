# Objective Correlative (客观对应物)

## One-liner
Externalize internal emotion through a concrete object, scene, or chain of events — make the reader feel without telling them what to feel.

## The Problem
"She was devastated." This tells the reader an emotion but doesn't produce it. The reader processes the information intellectually without feeling anything. Naming an emotion is the weakest way to convey it. The reader's imagination stays dormant.

## The Pattern

```
1. IDENTIFY the exact emotion the scene needs to produce
2. FIND a concrete, sensory object or situation that PRODUCES
   that emotion — not symbolizes it, produces it
3. EMBED the object in the scene without explanation
4. LET the reader's response to the object BE the emotion
5. TEST: remove all emotion-words. Does the reader still feel it?
```

## Techniques

**1. Object as emotion**
Bad: "She was grieving."
Good: "His reading glasses were still on the nightstand. She picked them up. The lenses still had his fingerprints."
(The specificity of fingerprints on a dead person's glasses — the reader does the emotional work.)

**2. Action as emotion**
Bad: "He was lonely."
Good: "He set two plates for dinner. Paused. Put one back. Then took it out again."
(The indecision is loneliness made physical.)

**3. Environment as emotion**
Bad: "The house felt threatening."
Good: "Every clock in the house had stopped at 3:47."
(Dread through one specific, wrong detail.)

**4. Sensory mismatch as emotion**
Bad: "She felt numb after the news."
Good: "The kettle was screaming. She watched it. After a while she made tea. It was already cold when she noticed it in her hand."
(Time distortion through sensory disconnect = numbness.)

**5. Accumulated correlatives**
For complex emotions, layer multiple objects that don't individually explain the feeling but together produce it.
"The dog kept going to the door. The mail was piling up. Someone had left flowers on the step, and they were beginning to turn."
(Absence made concrete through three objects. Each one alone is neutral. Together they devastate.)

## When to Use
- Any moment of strong emotion — grief, dread, love, rage, loneliness
- Climactic scenes where telling would undercut the impact
- When you catch yourself writing "felt," "realized," "was overcome by"
- Character interiority that needs to be shown through external reality

## Common Pitfalls
- **Symbols instead of correlatives**: A dove doesn't produce peace — it references it intellectually. A correlative must generate the emotion directly through its concrete reality.
- **Over-explaining the object**: "She picked up his glasses and felt a wave of grief." The second half kills the first. Trust the object.
- **Generic objects**: "Rain" for sadness is a dead correlative. The object must be specific enough to feel discovered, not deployed.
- **Forcing a single reading**: The best correlatives are slightly ambiguous — the reader's imagination fills in the emotional specifics, which makes the feeling more personal and powerful.

## Source
T.S. Eliot, "Hamlet and His Problems" (1919). Taught extensively in John Gardner's *The Art of Fiction* (1983) and Francine Prose's *Reading Like a Writer* (2006).
