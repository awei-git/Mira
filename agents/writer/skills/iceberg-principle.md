# The Iceberg Principle (冰山原则)

## One-liner
Know 100% of your story's world, show only 10-20% on the page — the unseen mass gives the visible surface its gravity.

## The Problem
Writers who do extensive worldbuilding or backstory often feel compelled to put all of it on the page. The result is exposition-heavy prose where the narrator stops to explain history, context, and motivation that the reader doesn't need (and didn't ask for). The story stalls under its own weight. Paradoxically, the more you explain, the less real the world feels — because real worlds don't come with footnotes.

## The Pattern

```
1. WRITE the full backstory for yourself — character history,
   world context, relationship dynamics, political situation
2. DRAFT the scene using your full knowledge
3. DELETE 80% of the exposition, leaving only what's load-bearing
4. TEST: does the remaining 20% imply the hidden 80%?
   Does the reader sense depth without seeing it?
5. LET character behavior, dialogue references, and environmental
   details do the exposition's job
```

## Techniques

**1. Behavior as backstory**
Bad: "She had been abused as a child, which made her flinch at sudden movements."
Good: "He reached across the table for the salt. She leaned back before she could stop herself."
(The flinch IS the backstory. The reader understands without being told.)

**2. Dialogue references to shared history**
Bad: "As you know, we met in college, where you betrayed my trust by stealing my research."
Good: "You promised you wouldn't do this again." "That was a long time ago." "Not long enough."
(The reader doesn't know what happened. They feel its weight.)

**3. Environmental shorthand**
Bad: "The factory had closed five years ago when the industry moved overseas, devastating the local economy and leaving half the workforce unemployed."
Good: "The parking lot still had faded lines, but grass was coming through the cracks."
(One image. The reader constructs the economic history.)

**4. The confident omission**
Skip the event everyone expects to see. A character receives devastating news — cut to the next morning. The reader's imagination fills in the night between, often more powerfully than you could write it.

**5. The small, unexplained thing**
Include a detail that implies a larger story but don't explain it.
"She always ordered two coffees. The second one she left to go cold."
(Who is the second coffee for? What happened to them? The reader doesn't need the answer. The question is the point.)

## The Diagnostic

Read your draft. Highlight every line of exposition, backstory, or explanation. For each one, ask:
1. Can the reader infer this from character behavior? If yes, cut.
2. Can the reader infer this from dialogue? If yes, cut.
3. Does the reader NEED this information right now? If not, cut.
4. Does keeping this make the world feel more real, or more explained? If explained, cut.

## When to Use
- Any scene where you've written more than two sentences of backstory
- Character introductions (resist the urge to give a biography)
- Worldbuilding in any genre (especially SF/fantasy, where the temptation to explain is strongest)
- Emotional revelations (let the reader feel, not be informed)

## Common Pitfalls
- **Iceberg with no visible tip**: If you cut TOO much, the reader has nothing to latch onto. The 20% that remains must be vivid, specific, and load-bearing.
- **Using it as an excuse for vagueness**: Hemingway's technique works because he KNEW the full story and wrote from that knowledge. Being vague because you haven't thought things through is not the iceberg principle — it's laziness.
- **Withholding information the reader genuinely needs**: The iceberg principle applies to backstory and context, not to essential plot information. If the reader needs a fact to understand the scene, provide it.
- **Confusing subtlety with obscurity**: The reader should feel the weight of what's unsaid. If they feel confused, you've cut too deep.

## Source
Ernest Hemingway, *Death in the Afternoon* (1932) and the Paris Review interview (1958): "If a writer of prose knows enough of what he is writing about he may omit things that he knows and the reader, if the writer is writing truly enough, will have a feeling of those things as strongly as though the writer had stated them." Extended by Charles Baxter in *The Art of Subtext* (2007).
