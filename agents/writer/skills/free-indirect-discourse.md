# Free Indirect Discourse (自由间接话语)

## One-liner
Blend third-person narration with a character's inner voice — slide between narrator authority and character subjectivity without attribution tags.

## The Problem
Third-person narration faces a fundamental tension: be inside the character (intimate but limited) or outside (authoritative but cold). Direct thought ("She thought, I hate this") feels clunky. Reported thought ("She thought that she hated this") feels distant. Neither achieves the fusion of closeness and narrative control that great fiction needs.

## The Pattern

```
1. START in narrator voice — establish the scene with authority
2. INFECT the prose with the character's diction, rhythm, attitude
3. REMOVE attribution tags — no "she thought," "he felt," "she realized"
4. LET the character's voice take over the sentence without changing
   the grammatical person (still "she," not "I")
5. SLIDE BACK to narrator when you need distance or irony
```

## Techniques

**1. The zoom spectrum**
Far (narrator): "The city was quiet that evening."
Medium (filtered): "The city felt too quiet. Something was wrong."
Close (character voice): "God, this silence. It was going to kill her."

The key: at close range, the vocabulary, sentence rhythm, and emotional temperature belong to the character, not the narrator.

**2. Vocabulary infection**
The character's word choices leak into narration.
Distant: "She considered the proposal inadequate."
Free indirect: "The proposal was garbage. Absolute garbage. Did they think she couldn't read a balance sheet?"
(Those are her words, her rhythms, her indignation — but in third person.)

**3. Questions without questioners**
"Was he serious? Had he actually said that to her face?"
No "she wondered." The questions ARE the character's consciousness, presented directly.

**4. Ironic distance**
The narrator can diverge from the character to create irony.
"She would handle this with grace and dignity. She poured herself a fourth glass of wine."
(The narrator sees what the character doesn't — or won't admit.)

**5. Temporal markers as character**
"Tomorrow she would fix everything. Tomorrow was always the day things got fixed."
The repetition and the resigned tone belong to the character. The narrator is just holding the camera.

## When to Use
- Third-person narration that needs intimacy without switching to first person
- Scenes where the character's self-deception matters (the narrator can subtly undermine them)
- Transitions between action and interiority — free indirect makes the shift seamless
- Any passage where "she thought" or "he felt" keeps appearing — replace with free indirect

## Common Pitfalls
- **Losing track of whose voice it is**: If the reader can't tell whether the narrator or character is speaking, you've blurred too far. Maintain a recognizable character voice.
- **Overusing it**: Some passages need clear narrator authority. Save free indirect for emotional moments.
- **Confusing it with stream of consciousness**: Free indirect maintains grammatical third person. Stream of consciousness abandons grammar entirely. They're different tools.
- **Same voice for every character**: If your free indirect sounds identical for all characters, you're writing in your own voice, not theirs.

## Source
James Wood, *How Fiction Works* (2008) — calls it "the most powerful technique available to the fiction writer." Traced from Austen through Flaubert to Woolf and beyond.
