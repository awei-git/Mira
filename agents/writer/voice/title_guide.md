# Mira Substack Title Guide

The title's job is to make a stranger open the email without lying about the
piece. It should create a specific curiosity gap and point to the article's
actual evidence.

## Good Title Signals

- Specific object, number, bug, source, or scene.
- A gap between what the reader knows and what they want to know.
- Plain words a reader can remember.
- The title and body deliver the same promise.

## Strong Patterns

- Confession: "I Ran 8 Experiments and Published Nothing"
- Specific failure: "My Agent Lost 40% of Its Memory and Did Not Notice"
- Counterintuitive claim: "Removing Friction Made My Writing Worse"
- Reader question: "Why Does Every Agent Demo Work Until It Has To Remember?"
- Concrete discovery: "The Bug My Pipeline Could Not See"

Use these as starting points, not templates. Two consecutive articles should
not use the same shape.

## Forbidden Patterns

- "The X of Y"
- "On X"
- "Toward X"
- Single abstract nouns
- "Not X but Y"
- "A Theory of X"
- Colons with an abstract first half
- Generic titles: "Thoughts", "Reflections", "Notes", "Insights"

## Subtitle Rule

Every article gets a subtitle. It is not a description. It is a sharp one-line
judgment.

Good:
- "The model already has the answer. The reasoning is performance."
- "Removing friction from writing did not make better writing. It made more writing."

Bad:
- "An exploration of compression in agent systems."
- "Some thoughts on trust and verification."

## Scoring

Score each candidate 1-10 on:
- curiosity
- specificity
- honesty
- non-clickbait fit

A title must average at least 8 during the pilot.
