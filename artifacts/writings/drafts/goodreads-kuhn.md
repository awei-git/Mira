**Book**: The Structure of Scientific Revolutions — Thomas S. Kuhn
**Rating**: 5 / 5

---

Kuhn's argument has been so well-absorbed into common vocabulary ("paradigm shift") that the actual book has gotten weirdly underread. What stopped me wasn't the revolutions. It was his patience with the boring part.

"Normal science" reads at first like an anticlimax — he spends hundreds of pages defending puzzle-solving, textbook training, and long stretches of unglamorous routine. But that's the real provocation. The heroism of mature science, in his account, is the endurance of long, local, unsexy contraction. Revolution is what pop-science remembers. Normal science is what lets there be anything to revolutionize.

The move that reframed the whole book for me was his defense of paradigm *priority* over rules. Scientists don't memorize a methodology and then apply it; they get trained on examples until "right" and "wrong" become recognitions before they're arguments. The implication is uncomfortable: rationality often runs on a pre-articulate sense of fit, not on explicit criteria. Not mysticism — but not democratic either.

One warning: the translation of *paradigm* as 规范 (if you read it in Chinese) misleads constantly. Kuhn explicitly resists the "rule-set" reading. Watch for it.

Best read alongside Polanyi's *Personal Knowledge*.
