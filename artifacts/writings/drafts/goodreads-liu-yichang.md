**Book**: 刘以鬯经典三部曲 (Liu Yichang: Three Classic Novels — 寺内 / 对倒 / 酒徒)
**Rating**: 5 / 5

---

The famous line from *Intersection* — "I am a slot machine, not a tiger" — isn't a clever personification. Most personifications make an object more human. Liu does the opposite: he pins the difference first, then lets the slot machine narrate anyway. The refusal itself is the voice.

What he's really doing, across all three novels, is handing the narrative chair to things that aren't supposed to have one. Stones, tram cars, tear gas canisters, newspapers. A riot seen by its debris. A city cross-cut into two strangers' parallel afternoons in *Tête-bêche*. An alcoholic writer in *The Drunkard* whose interior runs faster than the sentences can keep up with — and the prose strains to run with him, so the form breaks open.

Most experimental fiction burns out once you see the mechanism. Liu's doesn't, because the mechanism is always pointing at something outside itself — the disorientation of mid-century Hong Kong, the way cities chew people without storing them, the way modern consciousness is never unified enough to be reliably narrated. Technique as diagnosis.

If you only read one: *The Drunkard* for the voice, *Tête-bêche* for the structure, *Intersection* for the opening paragraph that never leaves your head.
