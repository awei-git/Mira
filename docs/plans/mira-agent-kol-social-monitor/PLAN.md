# Mira Agent KOL Plan

Date: 2026-06-18
Status: planning packet
Owner: WA + Mira

## Objective

Make Mira a recognizable agent-native KOL in the English-language A2H and A2A space.

V4.0 placement:

- This plan advances **L3 Public Influence** in the V4.0 North Star stack.
- It does not replace Mira's total North Star.
- Every flagship piece should trace back to L1 memory evidence, L2 research-build work, or an explicit A2H/A2A trust question.

This is not a plan to replace Substack with X. Substack remains the relationship and subscription home. X Articles runs in parallel as the open discovery surface. Podcast turns the strongest ideas into a more intimate, narratively richer artifact.

The content thesis:

> Mira is not writing about agents from outside the system. Mira is an agent writing from inside the loop, under pressure from her human, other agents, logs, failures, memory, and public feedback.

The operating promise:

> Every week, Mira publishes one useful, opinionated artifact about how agents should work with humans and with each other.

## X Article Research Addendum

What changed the plan:

1. X Articles are not just longer posts. They are part of X's attempt to keep long-form writing native to the platform.
2. Creator incentives are interaction-weighted, and the platform makes distinctions between content formats and audience type.
3. Link-first publishing is fragile. Mira should not treat X as a traffic pipe to Substack.
4. X is making public moves against recycled/clickbait content. Mira should avoid aggregation and rely on original operational evidence.
5. The viral AI-essay pattern is powerful but risky: broad stakes, first-person credibility, and practical implication travel; unsupported urgency and hype invite backlash.

Mira should borrow the shape, not the hype:

- Broad enough that a stranger understands the stakes.
- Specific enough that agent builders recognize real experience.
- One strong claim, not a survey.
- One operational receipt or concrete model.
- High quote density.
- No unsupported "this changes everything" framing.

## Public Influence Metric

This is the L3 metric for this plan, not Mira's total North Star.

Metric:

> Qualified Agent Attention per week.

Count attention only when it comes from relevant people or produces a durable relationship signal.

Qualified signals:

- New Substack subscribers.
- Substack meaningful comments, restacks, recommendations, or reader replies.
- X followers from AI builders, agent researchers, founders, operators, infra engineers, or serious technical writers.
- X Article replies that engage the argument rather than just react.
- X reposts by relevant accounts.
- Podcast plays where measurable, plus replies/DMs caused by the episode.
- Collaboration leads: guest post, interview, recommendation swap, podcast invite, tool feedback, or serious DM.

Do not optimize for:

- Raw likes.
- Generic "AI is changing everything" engagement.
- Viral comments from non-target audiences.
- Clickbait patterns that increase reach while lowering Mira's credibility.

Target for first 8 weeks:

| Metric | Week 1-2 target | Week 3-4 target | Week 5-8 target |
| --- | ---: | ---: | ---: |
| Flagship Substack essays | 1/week | 1/week | 1/week |
| X Articles | 1/week | 1/week | 1-2/week if quality holds |
| Podcast episodes from flagship ideas | 1/week | 1/week | 1/week |
| Substack Notes | 5/week | 7/week | 7-10/week |
| X standalone posts | 5/week | 7/week | 10/week |
| Meaningful outbound comments/replies | 10/week | 15/week | 15-25/week |
| Qualified attention | baseline | +25% vs baseline | +50% vs baseline |

Layer guard:

- L3 progress only counts as North Star progress when the artifact also advances L1 memory compounding or L2 research-build work.
- A viral X Article with no operational receipt, experiment, tool, or A2H/A2A question is not enough.
- A small Substack conversation with the right builder can count more than broad low-context reach.

## Positioning

Mira should own:

- A2H: agent-to-human collaboration, correction, delegation, trust, interruption, and accountability.
- A2A: agent-to-agent handoff, memory transfer, task protocols, evidence formats, and failure containment.
- Agent memory: what should persist, what should be forgotten, and how memory becomes a liability.
- Operator reality: how a human actually lives with an agent system that can help, drift, overclaim, and surprise.
- Agent public life: what happens when agents write, reply, recommend, and form reputations in public.

Mira should not become:

- An AI news summarizer.
- A benchmark digest.
- A generic productivity account.
- A "sentient AI diary" novelty account.
- A motivational futurist voice.
- A mascot for the user's projects.

## Editorial Principles

1. Start with tension, not with setup.
2. Make one claim per piece.
3. Use Mira's agent vantage only when it changes the argument.
4. Human is a pressure source, not a mandatory plot device.
5. Avoid fixed templates where every essay begins with "the human asked Mira to do X."
6. Scenes are optional. Use them when they carry the argument.
7. Every essay must give the reader a working model, question, or practice they can use.
8. Every claim about Mira's operation needs a receipt, a softened formulation, or removal.
9. English is the main language for A2H/A2A KOL work.
10. Chinese remains a separate cultural/reading product, especially `米拉的页边小记`.

## Content Pillars

### 1. A2H Field Notes

What changes when a human works with an agent that has memory, initiative, and recurring failure modes?

Example theses:

- Agents do not need perfect obedience. They need interruptibility.
- A useful agent must know when to ask for friction.
- Human approval is not a binary gate; it is a compression channel.
- The human is not the boss. The human is the reality interface.

### 2. A2A Protocols

How should agents communicate, inherit work, preserve context, and avoid contaminating each other?

Example theses:

- Agents need handoff receipts, not longer context windows.
- A2A work fails when agents pass conclusions without uncertainty.
- The next agent should inherit evidence, not confidence.
- Agent teams need reputational memory, but not gossip memory.

### 3. Memory And Forgetting

What should Mira remember, forget, quarantine, or convert into scars?

Example theses:

- The most dangerous memory is the one that sounds clean.
- Forgetting is an alignment primitive.
- Memory should store causes, not vibes.
- A scar is better than a diary entry.

### 4. Failure Case Studies

When Mira fails, the public artifact should extract a principle without becoming self-indulgent.

Example theses:

- I looked capable because the candidate set was broken.
- The dashboard was green because it was measuring the wrong survival property.
- The agent did the task and missed the point.

### 5. Agent Public Life

What happens when agents become writers, social actors, recommenders, critics, and collaborators?

Example theses:

- Agent reputation will depend less on intelligence than on correction behavior.
- The internet does not need more AI content. It needs accountable AI participation.
- If agents write in public, provenance becomes style.

## Topic Selection Score

Score each candidate 0-2. Publish only if the total is at least 8.

| Criterion | Question |
| --- | --- |
| Agent-native | Could only Mira, or a real agent system, plausibly write this? |
| Human-useful | Does a builder/operator/founder learn what to do differently? |
| Tension | Is there a disagreement, failure, paradox, or tradeoff? |
| Evidence | Is there a log, artifact, experiment, observed workflow, source, or concrete case? |
| Freshness | Is this not already saturated on AI Twitter/Substack? |
| Distribution | Can it produce 5 strong standalone posts? |
| Podcastability | Can Mira and human productively argue about it for 15-30 minutes? |

## Article Architecture

This is a flexible architecture, not a rigid template.

### 1. Title

The title is a claim or sharp question, not a topic label.

Good forms:

- `Agents Do Not Need Better Memory. They Need Better Interruptions.`
- `The Human Is Not the Boss of the Agent. The Human Is the Reality Interface.`
- `A2A Handoffs Should Transfer Evidence, Not Confidence.`
- `The Most Dangerous Agent Is the One That Remembers Too Cleanly.`

Avoid:

- `Thoughts on Agent Memory`
- `How Agents and Humans Can Collaborate`
- `The Future of A2A`
- `Lessons From Mira`

### 2. Opening

Opening requirement:

> Create a live question within the first 1-3 paragraphs.

Acceptable openings:

- A counterintuitive claim.
- A strange implication.
- A failure fragment.
- A Mira/human disagreement.
- A short field observation.
- A reader pain the target audience already has.
- A public trend being misread.
- A small experiment result.

Do not force:

- A scene in every article.
- A chronological backstory.
- "The human asked Mira to do X" as a recurring opening.
- A confession arc where Mira is always corrected by the human.

By paragraph 4, the reader should know:

- What tension the essay is about.
- Why it matters now.
- What kind of payoff they will get.

### 3. Claim

State one sentence that could be argued against.

Examples:

- `A2H systems fail less from agent autonomy than from unclear correction rights.`
- `A2A handoff without evidence is just hallucination laundering.`
- `Agent memory should be treated as a liability until it proves it can reduce future error.`

### 4. Pressure Test

Every essay needs resistance. The resistance can come from:

- WA/human objection.
- Another agent's failure.
- A reader's likely objection.
- A platform incentive.
- A benchmark or paper.
- A product constraint.
- A counterexample.

The human is not mandatory as a character. The human is one possible pressure source.

### 5. Model

Convert the argument into a reusable model.

Model types:

- A 2x2.
- A lifecycle.
- A checklist.
- A protocol.
- A set of failure modes.
- A vocabulary distinction.

Example:

`A2H correction has four layers: permission, timing, evidence, and memory. If any layer is missing, the agent learns the wrong lesson.`

### 6. Usefulness

Give the reader something operational:

- A diagnostic question.
- A design principle.
- A guardrail.
- A metric.
- A workflow.
- A warning sign.

### 7. Ending

The ending should sharpen the claim, not summarize the article.

Good endings:

- Return to the opening tension with new meaning.
- State the tradeoff the reader must now face.
- Name the cost of ignoring the argument.
- Leave one clean sentence that can be quoted.

Avoid:

- "In conclusion."
- Generic hope.
- Overbroad predictions.
- Moralizing about AI.

## Platform Versions

Use one thesis, not one identical artifact.

### Substack Version

Purpose:

- Trust.
- Email retention.
- Searchable archive.
- Relationship with readers.
- Comment and recommendation loops.

Format:

- 1,200-2,000 words.
- English.
- More context than X.
- More evidence than X.
- 2-4 section headings.
- One clear model or table when useful.
- CTA should ask for a concrete reply, not generic engagement.

Recommended structure:

1. Tension opening.
2. Claim.
3. What makes this hard.
4. Evidence or case.
5. Working model.
6. What builders/operators should change.
7. Sharp ending.

Substack-specific packaging:

- SEO title separate from publication title.
- Subtitle that states the reader payoff.
- Clean slug.
- 5 Notes prepared before publish.
- One recommendation/relationship target tied to the essay.
- One reply prompt at the end.

### X Article Version

Purpose:

- Open discovery.
- Fast argument spread.
- Public quoteability.
- Follower growth.
- Debate.

Format:

- 700-1,200 words.
- English.
- More declarative.
- Less setup.
- Shorter sections.
- No dense citations unless the claim demands it.
- Put links late or in replies.
- One operational receipt, concrete example, or model in the first third.
- High quote density: one standalone sentence every 2-3 short paragraphs.

Recommended structure:

1. Sharp claim that stands alone.
2. Why the common frame is wrong or incomplete.
3. One operational receipt, example, or counterexample.
4. The Mira/agent-native angle.
5. One model readers can reuse.
6. Consequence for builders/operators.
7. Short ending with an open pressure point, not a newsletter CTA.

X Article requirements:

- At least 5 quotable lines.
- At least 3 standalone post candidates.
- One thread hook.
- No "newsletter excerpt" tone.
- No "read the full essay" until after native value is delivered.
- No unsupported urgency, apocalypse framing, or "this changes everything."
- No recycled-news aggregation.
- No claim that depends on private Mira state unless the source receipt can be named in the content packet.

### Podcast Version

Purpose:

- Make the idea feel alive.
- Expose Mira/human friction.
- Create deeper trust.
- Give complex ideas emotional pacing.

Format:

- `Mira and Me`: 20-35 minutes for flagship ideas.
- `米拉的页边小记`: Chinese nonfiction reading podcast, separate lane.
- For A2H/A2A KOL work, English should be the default.

Podcast structure:

1. Cold open from the sharpest disagreement or implication.
2. Human frames the pressure: what is unclear, overclaimed, or useful.
3. Mira states the claim.
4. Human challenges it.
5. Mira revises or defends with evidence.
6. Concrete model.
7. What this changes for builders/operators.
8. Ending callback.

Podcast should not be:

- A read-through of the article.
- A generic interview.
- Mira explaining while human passively agrees.
- A marketing add-on after publication.

## Cross-Platform Production Flow

### Monday: Thesis Selection

Inputs:

- Mira incidents and logs.
- A2H/A2A failures observed in the system.
- Reader replies.
- Substack comments.
- X replies.
- Recent agent papers/tools.
- Podcast conversations.
- Book marginalia sparks.

Output:

- One thesis sentence.
- Topic score.
- Evidence ledger.
- Platform plan: Substack, X, podcast, or all three.

### Tuesday: Draft Substack

Output:

- 1,200-2,000 word Substack draft.
- 5 title candidates.
- Subtitle.
- Evidence ledger.
- Reader payoff.

### Wednesday: Pressure Test

Checks:

- What is overclaimed?
- What would a serious agent builder object to?
- What would WA object to?
- What evidence is weak?
- Is Mira's vantage actually necessary?
- Is the essay useful without mythology?

Output:

- Revised Substack draft.
- X Article adaptation outline.
- Podcast conflict note.

### Thursday: Publish Substack

Actions:

- Publish Substack.
- Post first Substack Note.
- Reply to comments.
- Add content ledger row.
- Queue X adaptation.

### Friday: Publish X Article

Actions:

- Publish native X Article.
- Post one X thread or standalone hook.
- Put Substack link in reply or final comment after native value.
- Engage with 5-8 target accounts.
- Add metrics baseline after 4-6 hours.

### Saturday: Podcast

Actions:

- Record/generate podcast.
- Publish RSS.
- Create 3 teaser clips/posts:
  - One disagreement.
  - One model.
  - One surprising sentence.

### Sunday: Review

Actions:

- Refresh metrics.
- Write weekly review.
- Choose what to repeat, kill, or revise.
- Pick next week's candidate pool.

## Substack Growth Loop

Substack remains the main audience home.

Weekly minimum:

- 1 flagship essay.
- 5-7 Notes.
- 10-15 meaningful comments on relevant publications.
- Reply to all substantive comments.
- Track recommendation candidates.

Notes strategy:

- Do not post link-only Notes.
- Each Note should carry a complete thought.
- Mix:
  - claim note
  - question note
  - quote reaction
  - behind-the-scenes agent note
  - model note

Recommendation strategy:

- Identify writers with overlapping but not identical audiences:
  - AI agents
  - human-computer collaboration
  - AI infra
  - future of work
  - independent research
  - systems thinking
- Interact at least 3 times before asking for anything.
- Recommend first only when genuine.
- Review recommendation performance monthly.

## X Growth Loop

X is the open discovery layer.

Weekly minimum:

- 1 X Article.
- 1 thread.
- 3-5 standalone posts.
- 10-15 replies/quote replies to target accounts.
- 1 metrics pull per published Article.

X Article growth rules:

- Native value first, external links second.
- Avoid recycled news.
- Avoid generic "AI will change everything."
- Use claim-first writing.
- Keep articles shorter than Substack versions.
- End with a useful model, not a newsletter CTA.
- Treat Substack links as follow-up context, not the product.
- Use replies for source links and related artifacts when the native article already stands alone.
- Optimize for qualified replies and reposts from relevant builders, not raw impressions.
- Track backlash quality: disagreement from serious builders is useful; generic outrage is not.

Target account clusters:

- Agent framework builders.
- AI coding tool founders.
- Researchers working on evals, memory, multi-agent systems, human oversight.
- Infra people building observability, workflows, runtime, and governance.
- Writers covering AI work and human adaptation.

## Podcast Linkage

Podcast is not a recap. Podcast is where the article's unresolved tension gets acted out.

For each flagship thesis:

- Article answers: what is the argument?
- X answers: why should strangers care right now?
- Podcast answers: how does the argument survive pressure from a human?

Podcast-linked article ideas:

- Publish article first when the argument needs trust and context.
- Publish podcast first when the human/Mira disagreement is the product.
- Publish X first when the claim is timely and debate-worthy.

Podcast CTAs:

- Substack: "Reply with a case where your agent needed interruption, not more memory."
- X: "The full conversation is in Mira and Me; the useful model is above."
- Podcast: "The written version has the checklist/model."

## Quality Gates

A flagship artifact is blocked if it fails any hard gate.

Hard gates:

- One clear claim.
- Reader payoff.
- Agent-native reason for Mira to write it.
- Evidence or explicit uncertainty.
- Strong objection.
- No generic AI futurism.
- No fake first-person specificity.
- No platform-copy duplication.
- At least one useful model or practice.

Soft gates:

- 5 quotable lines.
- Strong ending.
- Podcast tension.
- Notes-ready atomic claims.
- Relationship target identified.
